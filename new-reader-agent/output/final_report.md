## 🚨 Today's Lead Story
### Thailand joins UN-backed maritime process as Cambodia shifts border fight into legal arena
Thailand has agreed to take part in the UN-backed *compulsory conciliation* process launched by Cambodia to resolve their maritime boundary dispute, a notable step that pulls one of Southeast Asia’s most persistent bilateral conflicts into a formal international framework. Reuters reported that Bangkok will appoint conciliators and participate in the process after Phnom Penh initiated it earlier in the week. While the move does not settle the underlying sovereignty dispute, it signals that both capitals now see value in channeling the issue through legal and diplomatic mechanisms rather than allowing it to remain purely a matter of military posture or bilateral deadlock.

The significance of the development goes beyond the maritime claim itself. For years, the Cambodia-Thailand relationship has been shaped by overlapping land and sea disputes, periodic clashes, ceasefire crises and domestic nationalist pressure on both sides. Cambodia’s decision to invoke a little-used UN process reflects frustration with the limits of direct negotiation; Thailand’s decision to join suggests an interest in shaping the process from within and avoiding diplomatic isolation. In practical terms, this reduces the immediate risk of escalation and gives both governments a forum in which to argue their cases under international scrutiny.

Still, the underlying tensions remain unresolved. Reuters’ reporting makes clear that this is a legal and political contest, not a breakthrough. The mechanism can clarify positions and create momentum, but it is not a quick fix. For now, the maritime file has moved from the margins of the dispute to the center of regional attention—an indication that the Cambodia-Thailand conflict is becoming as much about institutions and process as it is about territory.

### Cambodian PM says Thailand still occupies ‘deep’ territory after ceasefire
Cambodian Prime Minister Hun Manet has accused Thailand of continuing to occupy parts of Cambodian territory despite ceasefire agreements intended to halt months of deadly border fighting. In comments reported by Al Jazeera from Reuters material, Hun Manet said Thai forces were positioned “deep into Cambodian territory in many areas” and had installed shipping containers and barbed wire in areas Cambodia considers its own. He said the barriers were preventing about 80,000 displaced people from returning home.

The report matters because it shows that the ceasefire has not translated into a settled frontier. Instead, the border remains contested in practical terms: who controls which stretch of ground, where barriers can be placed, and whether civilians can safely return. Al Jazeera also cited reporting and mapping that identified barriers in provinces including Pursat and Banteay Meanchey, reinforcing the sense that the conflict is still being managed through military positioning rather than resolved through demarcation.

Hun Manet called for the Joint Boundary Commission to accelerate technical border demarcation, underscoring a central theme of the conflict: without precise border definition, even ceasefires can become temporary pauses rather than durable peace. The story also highlights the role of outside actors, including the United States and China, in brokered truce efforts—evidence that regional diplomacy has helped dampen violence, but not settle the dispute.
